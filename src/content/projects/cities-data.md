The dashboard in Figure 1 shows that most trade quantities fall under
Appendix-II species, which are not necessarily threatened with extinction, but
the trades need to be closely controlled (UNEP & WCMC 2011). Contributions
from Appendix I and Appendix III are insignificant in this context (contributing
only 0.3% of total trade quantity throughout the years). This interesting view
suggests some potential insights behind the consistent centralised distribution
of trades under Appendix II species.

![alt text](/assets/CITES-endangered-species.png)
Figure 1. Endangered species trade quantities worldwide

For trades of Appendix II species, there was a huge drop in 2014 from the top
value in 2013, but it bounced back clearly in 2015, still just about half of the
amount in 2013 (shown in Figure 2). The movement indicates a potential
downtrend even though it is not steady throughout the years. From a
conservation specialist perspective, this is a good sign that wild species are
being traded less over time.

Beyond the trend, another analysis is conducted to gain deeper insights into the
highly centralised trade distribution under Appendix II species.

According to Figure 2, there are clear focuses on the international trades
regarding specific endangered genera, under Appendix II, being imported - the
top two genera (Gonystylus and Euphorbia) contribute more than 80% of
trades. On the other side, countries are importing endangered species much
more than the others - Switzerland stands for over 85% of trade, followed by
the Netherlands with only 5.18%.

![alt text](/assets/CITES-imported-species.png)
Figure 2. Appendix II Species imported and Countries importing worldwide.

The visualisation depicts that the trade domination of Appendix II species
focuses on a particular country (Switzerland) and genera (Gonystylus and
Euphorbia). It is interesting to further investigate to explore whether there is any
connection between Switzerland and the two genera.

Using another approach, a treemap is used to further break down the term
(product form) associated with each pair of country and genus. From Figure 3,
it is observable that there is a correlation between the top one importing
country (Switzerland), with the top two imported genera (Gonystylus,
Euphorbia). This explains that most trades were from Switzerland for the two
mentioned genera. This perfectly explains the relationship between the
importer country and the imported species.

Furthermore, it is recognisable that “timber pieces”, “wax”, and “timber” are
contributing to the top trades from Switzerland of the two identified genera
(which are plants). This fact shows that the big distances in trade quantities 
could be because of the nature of the product type, where there is high
demand in certain industries. According to Schafer (2022), Swiss timber-frame
construction is world-leading and growing, backed by research activity from
Swiss federal institutions. These plant-based products might have huge
markets which require many quantities.

![alt text](/assets/CITES-imported-species-by-terms.png)
Figure 3. Importing countries divided by Appendix II species and term
distribution

In general, the analysis identified the overall downtrend for international trade
of endangered species and which products, countries were importing the most
in the 2013-2015 period. Especially, it is notable that a big portion of trades was
focused on plant-based species, in the form of timber, wax, wood, etc. The
findings recommend that trade analysis needs to be more specific on context
rather than just focusing on the absolute number of trade quantities.
